# ZORECHO CUSTOMER EXPERIENCE CONSTITUTION

## Version 1.0 — Approved by James (CEO), July 23, 2026

The Engineering Constitution governs how we build software.
This constitution governs how customers experience it.
Both carry equal weight.

Every screen, workflow, feature, department, and integration must be evaluated
against one question:

**Can a busy business owner understand and use this in under one minute
without training?**

If the answer is no, the design is wrong. Not the customer. The design.

It evolves only through deliberate, CEO-approved amendments — never implicitly
through implementation.

---

# THE NON-NEGOTIABLE PRINCIPLES

## 1. Time to First Success
Every customer should achieve a meaningful business outcome within their first
hour. We do not measure onboarding by time to complete setup — we measure time
to first success. A customer who publishes their first post in 8 minutes stays
engaged, even if full setup takes 45. The flow is prioritized by "What's the
quickest way to let this business owner accomplish something they care
about?" — never by "What information do we need first?"

## 2. Echo Leads the Journey
Customers should never have to figure out where to go next — Echo guides every
step. Echo knows what is complete, what is missing, what is optional, what
blocks automation, and what should happen next. The customer never thinks
"Where do I go?" — they think "Echo is walking me through this."

## 3. One Decision at a Time
Never overwhelm customers with configuration screens or multiple choices when
one clear next action will do. Never present ten setup decisions
simultaneously. "Let's connect Facebook." Done. Next. Every completed step
immediately produces visible progress.

## 4. Every Click Creates Value
Navigation alone is not value; each interaction should accomplish something
meaningful. Connect account. Publish post. Generate ad. Import leads. Review
approvals. Hear briefing. Never: Settings. Advanced. Configuration.
Integration Manager. OAuth Console. Those concepts belong to engineers, not
business owners.

## 5. Progress Is Visible
Customers always know what's complete, what's next, and what each completed
step unlocks. Setup is experienced as unlocking abilities, not filling out
forms.

## 6. Business Language, Not Technical Language
Replace engineering concepts with outcomes customers understand. "Never miss a
lead" — not "IMAP integration". "Unlock automation" — not "OAuth grant".

## 7. No Hidden Dead Ends
If something blocks progress, explain why and how to fix it immediately — in
plain English, with the next action attached.

## 8. Customer Time Is Sacred
Every unnecessary click, screen, or minute is treated as a product defect, not
a training problem.

# THE CEO TEST

If the CEO, using the platform as an ordinary customer, becomes confused,
frustrated, or has to ask where something is, the experience fails. The
solution is to simplify the product — not to add documentation or training.
CEO frustration is valuable product feedback; friction removed before launch
benefits every future customer.

---

# THE FIRST HOUR

A brand-new customer's first hour is built around milestones, not
integrations. During it they should connect their business, connect their
accounts, publish real work, review their CRM, and hear Echo's first
briefing — with several moments of "Wow… it already did something useful,"
never "I think I'm almost configured."

- **Milestone 1 — Your First Win.** Before setup is finished, the customer
  accomplishes something real: publish a first post, build a first ad,
  generate a first email, or import a first lead.
- **Milestone 2 — Unlock Automation.** "To automate this, I need Facebook."
  Connect. Immediately show what that unlocks.
- **Milestone 3 — Never Miss a Lead.** Connect email. "I'm now watching your
  inbox."
- **Milestone 4 — Never Miss a Call.** Connect the phone agent. "I'll answer
  when you can't."
- **Milestone 5 — Business Ready.** Everything connected. Mission Control
  comes alive. Briefings, approvals, and automation begin.

The customer isn't filling out forms. They're unlocking abilities.

Most software asks: "What do we need from the customer?"
Zorecho asks: **"What can we do for the customer right now?"**
